LAND OF MILK & MEMORY
MARGARET THOMPSON / Content Creator

There is a tang in the air outside Snowville Creamery. It is the scent of Wednesday — yogurt day.

The cows of the neighboring Pomeroy farm are off grazing in a distant pasture, perhaps "a few hills over" as they like to say. In a couple hours the cows will be gathered in the dairy barn to be milked. Then they’ll march in a lackadaisical parade back into the fields. It’s an easy life being outside.

While the slopes of southeast Ohio isn’t the ideal location for a large-scale dairy processing plant, it’s the place that owner Warren Taylor calls home. His passion for the land and the memories it holds are strong.







After 30 years of designing dairy plants for some of the nation’s largest companies in California and Wisconsin, Warren was disenchanted by the industry. He decided to start his own small creamery around the corner from his home in Meigs County to do things a different way.

Snowville Creamery set up shop on the land near an existing dairy farm. They purchase raw milk from several local farmers to make “milk the way it used to be.”







Because Warren's attempts to run his business in a holistic way pit him against the large dairy industry, he has begun a grassroots effort to distinguish Snowville from the competition.

My Honest Table calls for dairy companies to label products from cows that are fed genetically modified grains. Further it urges Congress to pass legislation requiring this. To bring it to the attention of congressmen, Warren visits Washington D.C periodically to discuss the issue in their offices.
